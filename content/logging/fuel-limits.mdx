# Logging Fuel Limits

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Specific values — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channel/topic means

A fuel limit is the point where the fuel system can no longer deliver what the engine demands. It is not one channel but a pattern: high-side [rail pressure](/wiki/logging/hpfp-pressure) falling below target, low-side [LPFP pressure](/wiki/logging/lpfp-pressure) sagging, and/or [lambda](/wiki/logging/lambda-afr) going lean under load. Together these show the pumps/injectors are maxed out.

## Why it matters

Hitting a fuel limit under boost is dangerous: lean mixture plus boost is a fast path to knock and damage. It is also a hard ceiling on power — no calibration can pull more fuel than the hardware can supply. Recognizing a fuel limit tells you to **stop and add capacity**, not to keep demanding fuel.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- Rail pressure tracks target through the whole pull.
- LPFP supply steady.
- Lambda holds the commanded target.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **Rail pressure crashing** as demand peaks → HPFP at its limit ([HPFP Failure](/wiki/diagnostics/hpfp-failure), [Fuel Pressure Crash](/wiki/diagnostics/fuel-pressure-crash)).
- **LPFP pressure sagging first** → low side is the limit ([LPFP Failure](/wiki/diagnostics/lpfp-failure)).
- **Lambda going lean** at the same moment → the dangerous combination; stop.
- Limit appearing after a **high-ethanol fill** → blend needs more volume than the system supports.

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Reading rail pressure **without lambda** (or vice versa) and missing the danger.
- Blaming the HPFP when the **LPFP** sagged first.
- Treating a fuel limit as a tuning value to dial around instead of a hardware upgrade.
- Pushing a high-ethanol blend the fuel system was not built for.
