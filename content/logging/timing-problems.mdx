# Logging Timing Problems

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Acceptable amounts — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channel/topic means

Timing problems are read from the [timing-correction](/wiki/logging/timing-corrections) channels: how much advance the DME is pulling (negative) in response to knock detection, ideally **per cylinder**. This page is about interpreting that pull — what causes it and how to tell a whole-engine cause from a single-cylinder one.

## Why it matters

Knock is destructive. Timing pull is the engine protecting itself, and reading it correctly is how you keep a pull safe and find the real cause. Sustained or growing pull means the fuel/heat/hardware/tune combination is at or past its limit.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- Little to no timing pulled under load.
- Any small corrections are brief and recover.
- All cylinders behave similarly.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **Sustained negative corrections** under load → knock; check fuel grade, ethanol content, heat.
- **Pull growing with [IAT](/wiki/logging/iat)** → heat-driven ([Heat Soak](/wiki/cooling-oil/heat-soak)).
- **One cylinder pulling much more** → cylinder-specific hardware (plug, coil, injector), not the whole tune.
- Pull alongside **lean [lambda](/wiki/logging/lambda-afr) / [rail-pressure crash](/wiki/logging/hpfp-pressure)** → fuel-supply-driven knock.

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Reacting to one tiny brief correction vs a repeatable trend.
- Reading averaged timing instead of **per-cylinder** — you miss a single bad cylinder.
- Ignoring **IAT and fuel** context, then blaming the base tune for heat- or fuel-driven knock.
- Pulling timing in the calibration to mask knock from bad plugs or bad fuel — that hides the real fault.
