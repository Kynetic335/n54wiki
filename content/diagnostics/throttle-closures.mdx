# Throttle Closures (Diagnosis)

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (behavior). Limiter values are car/tune dependent — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the symptom means

The N54 uses a torque-based control model: the pedal asks for torque, and the DME opens the throttle (and manages boost/fuel/spark) to deliver it. A **throttle closure** is when the DME **closes the throttle even though the pedal is still down**, because something told the torque model to back off. The driver feels a hesitation, surge, or cut mid-pull.

A closure is the DME doing its job. The diagnostic question is *what asked for less torque* — protection, a limiter, or a fault.

## Common causes

- **Protective intervention** — knock/timing pull, high IAT, overheating, or fuel-pressure problems making the DME reduce torque.
- **Torque limiter reached** — calibration cap on requested/delivered torque (including drivetrain protection, e.g. automatic transmission limits).
- **Load/boost ceiling** in the calibration.
- **Sensor fault** feeding the torque model bad data (throttle/pedal/airflow).
- **Component fault** triggering torque reduction.

## What to log

- [Throttle angle](/wiki/logging/throttle-angle)
- Pedal position
- Torque request (if exposed)
- [Load requested vs actual](/wiki/logging/load-requested-vs-actual)
- Supporting safety channels: [timing corrections](/wiki/logging/timing-corrections), [IAT](/wiki/logging/iat), [rail pressure](/wiki/logging/hpfp-pressure)

## What a normal log pattern looks like

- Throttle angle tracks pedal demand and stays open through a WOT pull.
- Requested and actual load rise together.
- No sudden throttle drop while the pedal is flat.

## What a bad log pattern looks like

- **Throttle angle dropping while pedal stays at 100%** — the signature of a closure.
- A closure that lines up with a **protective trigger** (timing pull, high IAT, rail-pressure drop) → protection, fix that cause.
- A closure with **no protective trigger**, at a repeatable torque/load point → likely a **limiter** in the calibration or a drivetrain cap.

## Mechanical checks first

1. Check the **safety channels** for what triggered protection (knock, heat, fuel pressure).
2. Rule out **sensor faults** (throttle body, pedal, airflow) and scan for codes.
3. Confirm the engine is otherwise healthy before assuming a software limiter.

## Tuning-related causes second

Only after protection and sensor faults are ruled out: a closure at a steady torque/load ceiling is usually a calibration limiter. These exist for protection (including transmission/drivetrain). Changing limiters affects safety margins and driveline stress and must be done with full understanding of why the limit exists — never blindly to "remove a cut."

## When to stop tuning

Stop if closures are protection-driven (knock, heat, fuel) — fix the root cause first. Do not raise or remove limiters to chase a closure you have not explained.
