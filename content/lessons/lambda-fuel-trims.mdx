# Lambda / AFR and Fuel Trims

Lambda is the ratio of actual air-fuel mixture to stoichiometric. On the N54, **lambda target** drives high-load enrichment strategy, and **short-term / long-term fuel trims (STFT/LTFT)** reveal how hard the closed-loop correction system is working to compensate for fuel model error.

## What This System Does

### Lambda at high load

Under wide-open throttle, the N54 runs a commanded lambda target richer than stoichiometric to manage combustion temperature, knock margin, and turbine heat. The exact target depends on boost level, fuel type, and ethanol content.

The DME compares commanded to measured lambda and uses fuel scalar and injection mass to close the gap. A lean miss at high load is an engine risk — not a calibration inconvenience.

### Fuel trims at light load and cruise

At idle and cruise, the closed-loop system uses STFT (fast corrections, resets frequently) and LTFT (slow accumulation, persistent) to center fueling around stoichiometric. Healthy trims are small and balanced between banks.

### Fuel scalar

The **fuel scalar** corrects for fuel energy content and injector mass-flow assumptions. On ethanol blends, raising the scalar accounts for ethanol's lower energy density so the same injection duration delivers the correct fuel mass for the commanded lambda.

---

## Why It Matters

Ignoring trims before a calibration change is the most common fueling mistake. A car with large positive LTFTs on pump gas has a fuel model already running lean — adding boost or ethanol without addressing the root cause stacks risk. Scalar adjustments are car-specific; they do not transfer between builds.

## What Logs Prove

- `Lambda requested (bank 1/2)` — commanded target
- `Lambda actual (bank 1/2)` — measured by wideband O2
- `STFT bank 1/2 (%)` — current closed-loop correction; should be near zero at cruise
- `LTFT bank 1/2 (%)` — accumulated correction; large values mean persistent fuel model error
- `Ethanol content (%)` — confirms blend (with FlexFuel sensor)
- `HPFP pressure actual` — pressure drops produce lean symptoms that look like trim problems

### Healthy trim pattern
- STFT oscillating within a few percent of zero at cruise
- LTFT near zero — model is already close
- Bank 1 and bank 2 trims within a few percent of each other

### Problem patterns

| Pattern | Likely cause |
|---------|-------------|
| Both banks LTFT strongly positive | Scalar too low for blend, or injector wear |
| One bank LTFT positive, one neutral | Bank-specific injector or O2 issue |
| Trims swing large under load | HPFP pressure drop — pressure problem, not scalar |
| LTFT good on pump gas, goes lean on ethanol | Scalar not updated for blend change |

## Safe Adjustment Direction

1. Fix hardware first — verify injectors, O2 sensors, and fuel pressure before touching scalar
2. Confirm ethanol content with a live log channel — never assume blend
3. Make scalar changes in small steps and relog before going further
4. Never use scalar to hide a fuel pressure problem — symptoms look similar but the cause is different

---

## Danger Signs

- Lambda actual leans out under WOT — target not met under boost
- Trims pegged at their positive limit — model is far off and cannot self-correct
- Bank-to-bank trim split beyond several percent — hardware asymmetry, not scalar
- LTFT normalizes after fuel pressure repair — confirms pressure was the root cause
