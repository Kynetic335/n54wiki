# Logging Coolant Temp

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Specific thresholds — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channel means

Coolant temperature is the engine's primary temperature reading. The DME uses it for fueling, for protection thresholds, and to decide when to reduce power or enter limp mode if the engine gets too hot. On the N54 the electric water pump and thermostat manage it, and both are known wear items.

## Why it matters

Coolant temp tells you whether the cooling system is keeping up and whether heat is the reason for a power complaint. Climbing coolant temps under sustained load can bring on timing pull and protective cuts, and a sudden climb can mean a failing water pump — a stop-now condition to avoid heat damage.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- Coolant temp warms to operating range and holds steady.
- Stable under load with adequate cooling.
- Recovers normally after a pull.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **Climbing coolant temp under load** → cooling-system limit (radiator, water pump, thermostat, airflow).
- **Sudden spike** → possible water-pump failure; stop. See [Water Pump Failure](/wiki/cooling-oil/water-pump-failure).
- Slow warm-up / never reaching range → stuck-open thermostat.
- Power loss/timing pull tracking high coolant temp → heat-driven protection.

## Which diagnostics use this channel

- [Limp Mode](/wiki/diagnostics/limp-mode) (overheat protection)
- [Timing Corrections (Diagnosis)](/wiki/diagnostics/timing-corrections) (heat context)
- Cooling faults (see [Water Pump Failure](/wiki/cooling-oil/water-pump-failure))

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Treating heat-driven power loss as a tuning fault.
- Ignoring a rising trend until it becomes an overheat.
- Confusing coolant temp with [IAT](/wiki/logging/iat) or [oil temp](/wiki/logging/oil-temp) — different signals.
- Pulling hard on a not-yet-warm or already-hot engine.
