# Logging IAT

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Specific thresholds — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channel means

IAT (intake air temperature) is the temperature of the air entering the engine after the turbos and intercooler. On a TMAP-equipped setup the same sensor may also report manifold pressure. Hotter intake air is denser in energy but more prone to knock, so the DME gets more conservative (pulls timing) as IAT climbs. **Verify sensor type/scaling by VIN/ROM ID**, especially on cars converted to an N20 TMAP or higher-range sensor.

## Why it matters

IAT is the context that explains a lot of "why did it make less power" questions. Heat soak — from repeated pulls, a weak intercooler, or a hot engine bay — drives IAT up, which drives timing pull, which costs power. Without IAT in the log you can misread a heat problem as a tuning problem.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- IAT reasonable for ambient conditions and not climbing hard during a single pull.
- Recovers between pulls with adequate cooldown.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **IAT climbing pull-to-pull** → heat soak; let the car cool, improve intercooling. See [Heat Soak](/wiki/cooling-oil/heat-soak).
- **Timing pull that tracks rising IAT** → heat-driven knock, not a base-tune fault. See [Timing Corrections](/wiki/diagnostics/timing-corrections).
- Implausible IAT (very high/low/stuck) → sensor or **wrong scaling** after a sensor conversion.

## Which diagnostics use this channel

- [Timing Corrections (Diagnosis)](/wiki/diagnostics/timing-corrections)
- [Timing Problems](/wiki/logging/timing-problems)
- Heat-related power loss (see [Heat Soak](/wiki/cooling-oil/heat-soak))

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Comparing pulls at **different IAT** and blaming the tune for the hotter one.
- Ignoring IAT when reading timing corrections.
- Trusting IAT after a **TMAP/sensor swap** without confirming scaling.
- Expecting a single number to be "good/bad" without ambient context.
