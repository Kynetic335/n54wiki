# HPFP / LPFP Fuel Pressure

The N54 uses a **two-stage fuel system**: a low-pressure pump (LPFP) in the tank feeds a high-pressure direct-injection pump (HPFP) on the engine. Both must operate correctly for the fuel system to support power and ethanol targets. A pressure failure at either stage looks like a fueling problem in the log — but the fix is hardware, not calibration.

## What This System Does

### Low-pressure circuit (LPFP)

The in-tank LPFP supplies fuel to the HPFP at a regulated low pressure. On a stock N54, the LPFP is a known weak point at high ethanol blends and sustained high-load operation. If LPFP supply drops, the HPFP cannot maintain rail pressure regardless of its own mechanical condition.

### High-pressure circuit (HPFP)

The HPFP is a cam-driven direct-injection pump that raises fuel pressure from the LPFP supply level to the high rail pressure the injectors need. The MSD80/81 commands a **fuel pressure target** and the HPFP tracks it. Rail pressure is logged as `HPFP pressure actual`.

### How pressure affects calibration

At high ethanol blends, more fuel mass is needed per injection event. This increases HPFP demand. If pressure cannot hold target, injectors cannot deliver the commanded fuel mass — the result is lean lambda, positive trims, and eventually misfire. This sequence is routinely misread as a scalar or tune problem.

---

## Why It Matters

Fuel pressure problems mimic calibration errors. A lean lambda reading under load might be a scalar issue — or it might be the fuel system physically unable to deliver the commanded fuel mass. Only a log with both pressure and lambda channels captured together can distinguish them.

---

## What Logs Prove

Log these channels on every fuel system diagnostic pull:

- `HPFP pressure actual (bar)` — actual rail pressure
- `HPFP pressure target (bar)` — commanded pressure
- `LPFP pressure (bar)` or `LPFP voltage (V)` — supply side health
- `Lambda actual (bank 1/2)` — leans out when pressure drops
- `STFT bank 1/2 (%)` — positive trims follow a pressure sag

### Diagnostic sequence in the log

LPFP drops → HPFP loses prime → HPFP actual falls below target → lambda leans → STFT goes positive → misfire possible.

If HPFP drops first without LPFP dropping, the HPFP itself is the weak point.

## Safe Adjustment Direction

1. Log baseline LPFP and HPFP together across multiple pulls before any calibration change
2. If pressure holds target — hardware is adequate for current demand
3. If pressure sags — reduce load or ethanol demand until hardware is upgraded
4. Do not raise HPFP pressure target on a struggling pump — it increases wear without fixing supply deficit

---

## Danger Signs

- HPFP actual falls away from target during WOT
- LPFP drops before HPFP — tank supply is the bottleneck
- Lambda leans out and STFT goes positive immediately after a pressure sag
- Rail pressure oscillates instead of holding steady — pump at or beyond capacity
- Long crank after repeated hard pulls — pump cavitating
