# Logging Lambda / AFR

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Target values — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channel means

This channel reports the air-fuel mixture. **Lambda** is normalized: 1.0 = exactly stoichiometric for whatever fuel is burning, below 1.0 = rich, above 1.0 = lean. **AFR** is the same idea expressed for a specific fuel (so the stoichiometric AFR number changes with fuel). Lambda is the safer way to talk mixture across gasoline and ethanol blends because it does not depend on the fuel. On the N54 the source may be the factory oxygen sensors and/or an added wideband — confirm what your tool reads. **Verify by VIN/ROM ID.**

## Why it matters

Mixture under load is a primary safety reading. The N54 typically commands a richer (below 1.0) mixture under boost for knock and thermal safety. A mixture that goes **lean under boost** raises knock and damage risk fast, so lambda is something you watch live, not just review later.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- Lambda sits at the commanded target through the pull (commonly richer under boost).
- Smooth, plausible response — not stuck or noisy.
- Banks read similarly.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **Lean under boost** → fuel-supply limit ([Fuel Pressure Crash](/wiki/diagnostics/fuel-pressure-crash)), wrong ethanol blend, or a leak; **stop**.
- **Rich beyond target** → leaking injector or sensor error ([Rich Condition](/wiki/diagnostics/rich-condition)).
- **Stuck / slow / implausible lambda** → suspect the [O2 sensor](/wiki/diagnostics/o2-sensor-faults), not the fueling.
- One bank off vs the other → injector or sensor on that bank.

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Comparing **AFR numbers across different fuels** — use lambda to stay fuel-agnostic.
- Trusting lambda from a **lazy/failed O2 sensor**.
- Reading lambda **without rail pressure** — you miss whether a lean spike was a fuel-supply crash.
- Reacting to one noisy sample instead of a trend.
