# Logging STFT / LTFT

> **Source confidence:** High confidence (concept). Acceptable trim ranges — verify by VIN/ROM ID.

## What the channels mean

Fuel trims are the corrections the DME applies to hit its mixture target. **STFT** (short-term fuel trim) reacts quickly to live feedback; **LTFT** (long-term fuel trim) is the learned, slower-moving correction the DME settles into over time. A trim near zero means the base fueling is close to right. A large positive trim means the DME is **adding** fuel (it saw lean); a large negative trim means it is **pulling** fuel (it saw rich).

## Why it matters

Trims reveal problems the raw lambda number alone might hide: vacuum leaks, fueling drift, a failing sensor, or injector issues. They are most useful at idle and steady cruise, where closed-loop fueling is active and trims tell you what the engine is fighting.

## What normal behavior generally looks like

- STFT and LTFT small and stable.
- Banks (if separate) read similarly.
- No big swing when the engine is warm and steady.

## What abnormal behavior can suggest

- **Large positive trims** → lean correction: unmetered air (vacuum/PCV leak) or weak fueling. See [Lean Condition](/wiki/diagnostics/lean-condition), [Rough Idle](/wiki/diagnostics/rough-idle).
- **Large negative trims** → rich correction: leaking injector or sensor error. See [Rich Condition](/wiki/diagnostics/rich-condition).
- **One bank's trims very different** → injector or [O2 sensor](/wiki/diagnostics/o2-sensor-faults) on that bank.
- Trims drifting after a fuel change → blend/ethanol mismatch.

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Reading trims **at WOT** to judge fueling — closed-loop trims are an idle/cruise tool; use [lambda](/wiki/logging/lambda-afr) for WOT.
- Treating a large trim as a tuning value to dial out instead of finding the leak/injector/sensor causing it.
- Ignoring **bank-to-bank** differences that localize the fault.
- Judging trims on a cold or not-yet-settled engine.
